Philosophy of Science

The Rope Ladder
of Explanation

Where description turns into explanation, and explanation into meta-interpretation. A walk through the stages of scientific understanding.

Theorist climbing; empirical adjudication stabilising each rung.

What does it mean to explain something? The question sounds innocent, but it fractures on contact with practice. We observe, we classify, we notice patterns, we write equations, we embed those equations in theoretical frameworks. At what point in this ascent does mere description become genuine explanation? And does the ascent ever end, or does the ladder disappear into mist?

These are not idle puzzles. The history of physics is a history of climbing, and the rungs are not always stable. Locke's naive empiricism would strand us on the lowest rungs, insisting that knowledge begins and ends in sensory experience. But the deepest laws of physics invoke entities no one has ever seen: curved spacetime, gauge fields, wavefunctions. The ladder leads upward into abstraction, and the question of whether the upper rungs describe reality or merely organise our predictions remains open.

The simplest compression of this ascent is a triad: Observe, Describe, Explain. But the triad hides important structure. Between raw observation and deep explanation lie distinct stages, each demanding its own intellectual commitment. The ladder spreads out what the triad compresses.

A First Climb

The anomalously large supermoon

Before we formalise the ladder, let us climb it once using something familiar. You step outside on a clear evening and notice the Moon hanging low on the horizon. It appears enormous — far larger than it ever seems when high overhead. You are told it is a supermoon. But is it really bigger? And what would count as knowing? Watch how the simple triad — Observe, Describe, Explain — expands into five distinct intellectual commitments.

A supermoon near the horizon. The physical increase in angular size is modest; the perceptual enlargement can be dramatic.
1
Percept Observe
An experience presents itself
A large, luminous disc rests above the rooftops. It looks striking — implausibly large. At this stage there is no classification, no measurement, no inference. There is only the experience itself. It is immediate, private, and unanalysed.
2
Fact Describe
The experience is identified
The object is recognised as the Moon. More specifically, it is a supermoon: a full Moon occurring near perigee, its closest orbital approach to Earth. Its angular diameter is roughly 14% greater than at apogee. This is now a public claim — testable and confirmable. Yet the Moon appears far more than 14% larger. The appearance exceeds the astronomical difference.
3
Regularity Describe (patterned)
A stable correlation is noticed
The Moon consistently appears larger near the horizon than when high in the sky, whether or not it is at perigee. This is the Moon illusion. The pattern is repeatable and intersubjective: low elevation correlates with perceived enlargement. But correlation is not explanation.
4
Law Explain
Quantitative relations constrain the case
Celestial mechanics allows the Moon’s true angular diameter to be calculated from orbital parameters: about 0.56° at perigee, 0.49° at apogee. A photograph confirms that the retinal image changes only modestly. The dramatic horizon enlargement is therefore perceptual rather than optical. Now explanation begins.
5
Theory Explain (deepen)
The phenomenon is embedded within a framework
The Moon illusion is interpreted within broader theories of visual perception. Apparent size is linked to perceived distance and contextual cues processed by the visual system. The supermoon’s apparent enormity becomes a composite: a modest astronomical increase combined with a systematic perceptual effect. The theory explains not only this case, but a family of size-perception phenomena.
Consider the ascent. We moved from private experience to public description, from description to patterned regularity, from regularity to quantitative constraint, and from constraint to theoretical unification. Each step traded immediacy for generality. Induction carried us upward — from this Moon on this evening to claims about all observers under comparable conditions.
Voices on the Ladder

What the climbers said

Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
Carl Sagan

Sagan's caution cuts to the heart of inductive reasoning. We climb the ladder by generalising from observed cases, but the next rung might not hold. The absence of a counterexample does not prove a law; it merely records our ignorance so far. Every empirical regularity, however well confirmed, remains provisional. The question is whether the structural principles at the top of the ladder, the symmetries and conservation laws, are themselves immune to this worry, or whether they too are merely patterns we have not yet seen broken.

The premises in any scientific explanation should be better known than what was to be explained.
Aristotle, Posterior Analytics

Aristotle's requirement is deceptively simple: you explain the less known by the better known. But what counts as "better known"? On the ladder, this means the higher rungs must have earned their status through repeated empirical testing. A law used as an explanatory premise must itself have evolved to the status of well-established description before it can serve as the foundation for explaining something new. The premises must have been climbed to, not assumed.

What would it have looked like if the sun went around the earth?
Ludwig Wittgenstein (attributed), on theory-ladenness

The question is devastating because the answer is: it would have looked exactly the same. The sun crossing the sky is the percept, rung 1. The Ptolemaic and Copernican systems both save the phenomena at rung 3. The difference emerges only at rungs 4 and above, where dynamical laws, simplicity, and unifying structure separate the two accounts. Observation alone cannot adjudicate. This is the theory-ladenness of observation: what you see depends on the theoretical framework you bring to the looking. The ladder is not a neutral staircase; the upper rungs reshape how you interpret the lower ones.

Science's truths were true before there were societies; will still be true after all philosophers are dead; were true before any minds, even trilobite or dinosaur minds, to notice them.
Richard Dawkins

Science is not a social construct. The inverse-square law did not begin when Newton wrote it down. The challenge Dawkins poses to the ladder metaphor is this: the rungs we climb are epistemic, they track our ascent through understanding, but the structure they approximate exists independently of the climbing. The laws were there before the ladder was built. This is the realist's conviction: that the upper rungs, however abstract, correspond to something in the world, not merely in our heads.

Does it make sense to think that physical laws themselves might evolve?
Carlo Rovelli

Rovelli's question destabilises the entire ladder. If laws evolve, then what we took to be rung 6 or 7, structural principles, timeless constraints, might actually be provisional rung 4 or 5. The distinction between operational law and structural principle would itself be historically contingent. The ladder would not end; it would fold back on itself, with today's deepest framework becoming tomorrow's effective description. This is not mere scepticism. It is a live question in quantum gravity, where the very structure of spacetime may be emergent rather than fundamental.

The Chain

Percept, Fact, Regularity, Law, Theory

A particular experience is first noticed, then named. Repeated instances stabilise into a pattern. The pattern is expressed as a quantitative relation. The relation is finally embedded within a framework that explains why it must take the form it does.

But this familiar ascent conceals a critical question: when does explanation actually begin? Regularity is not explanation. Correlation tells us what tends to happen. A law tells us what must happen, given these quantities and relations. Theory tells us why that law holds at all.

Do we need a mechanism to explain causation? What is it to describe the movement of things? Newton could predict planetary orbits without explaining what gravity is. His famous hypotheses non fingo declared the operational law sufficient. Einstein later provided the mechanism: spacetime curvature. But even Einstein's field equations require constitutive closure, boundary conditions, a specified regime, before they explain any particular phenomenon.

The Expanded Ladder

Seven rungs from percept to framework

The familiar five-step ladder needs refinement. A fundamental law by itself is formally powerful but empirically indeterminate. It does not explain anything concrete until supplied with constitutive relations, boundary conditions, and domain restrictions. The expanded ladder inserts the rungs where explanation actually becomes determinate.

1
Perceptual Encounter
Notice / Detect
A specific experience, indexical and situated. A pattern appears in the visual field. No classification yet. "There is a luminous arc in the telescope image."
No explanation
2
Public Description
Name / Classify
The object is identified within a shared vocabulary. A reportable fact enters the scientific record. "This is an Einstein ring." "That is a hydrogen absorption spectrum."
Describe
3
Regularity
Generalise / Correlate
Repeated association across cases. "Massive objects systematically deflect light." "Stars of certain temperatures display characteristic spectral lines." Regularity answers: what tends to happen?
Patterned description
4
Operational Law
Explain / Predict
A quantitative relation between measurable variables, predictive within a defined regime. The lens equation relates deflection angle to mass and impact parameter. The Rydberg formula predicts discrete wavelengths. This is where explanation genuinely begins, but it is still domain-bound.
Explanation begins
5
Constitutive Closure
Specify / Close
The system is closed. We specify what the medium is, how it responds, its internal parameters. Equations of state plus boundary conditions make prediction determinate. Without this rung, even fundamental equations remain empty: grammar without vocabulary.
Explanation becomes determinate
6
Structural Principle
Constrain / Derive
Symmetry principles, field equations, conservation laws, invariance constraints. These do not by themselves explain particular phenomena. They constrain what any admissible empirical law may look like. The Bianchi identities enforce conservation. Gauge invariance constrains interaction terms.
Meta-explanation
7
Unifying Framework
Interpret / Re-express
Multiple laws embedded under one structure. Abstract entities, fields, potentials, metrics, show deep connections across domains. Gravitational lensing, orbital precession, and gravitational waves all emerge as consequences of spacetime curvature described by gμν.
Coherence across domains

The Ascent: Observation to Geometric Necessity

1
Percept: A luminous arc appears in the telescope image, encircling a darker central object.
2
Fact: The image is classified as an Einstein ring: light from a distant source distorted by an intervening mass.
3
Regularity: Massive foreground objects systematically produce arcs and multiple images of background sources.
4
Operational law: The lens equation, α = 4GM/c²b, relates the deflection angle to the mass M and impact parameter b. It predicts the angular radius of the ring.
5
Constitutive closure: Specify the mass distribution of the lens (point mass, isothermal sphere, NFW profile), the source-lens-observer geometry, and the cosmological distances. Now the prediction is determinate.
6
Structural principle: Einstein's field equations, Rμν − ½Rgμν = 8πGTμν, constrain how mass-energy curves spacetime. The lens equation is derived from geodesic motion in this curved geometry.
7
Unifying framework: The same metric tensor gμν that explains lensing also explains orbital precession, gravitational waves, and cosmological expansion. Lensing is one face of a single geometric structure.

The Ascent: Colour to Gauge Symmetry

1
Percept: A pattern of coloured lines appears in dispersed starlight.
2
Fact: The star exhibits a hydrogen absorption spectrum. Dark lines at specific wavelengths are identified and catalogued (Fraunhofer lines).
3
Regularity: Stars of certain surface temperatures display characteristic spectral line patterns. Hotter stars show helium lines; cooler stars show molecular bands.
4
Operational law: The Rydberg formula, 1/λ = R(1/n² − 1/m²), predicts discrete wavelengths for hydrogen transitions. It is exact within its domain.
5
Constitutive closure: Specify the atomic species, ionisation state, electron density, and temperature. The Saha equation and Boltzmann distribution close the system, making line strengths and profiles determinate.
6
Structural principle: Quantum mechanics explains why energy levels are discrete. The Schrodinger equation for the Coulomb potential yields the Rydberg formula as an exact consequence. Gauge invariance (U(1) symmetry) constrains the electromagnetic interaction.
7
Unifying framework: Quantum electrodynamics embeds atomic spectra, the photoelectric effect, Compton scattering, and Lamb shift corrections within a single renormalisable field theory.
Mercury's Perihelion
From orbital anomaly to differential geometry
An anomalous shift → quantified residual against Newton → a revised dynamical law → Einstein's field equations → the Bianchi identities enforce conservation.
Expand the full ladder

The Ascent: Newton to Einstein to Bianchi

1
Percept: Mercury's closest approach to the Sun does not return to the same point each orbit. The ellipse rotates.
2
Fact: The anomalous precession is quantified: 43 arcseconds per century remain after accounting for perturbations from other planets.
3
Regularity: The residual is persistent, systematic, and consistent across observations spanning decades. It is not noise; it is a stable deviation from Newtonian prediction.
4
Operational law: The Schwarzschild solution to Einstein's field equations yields an effective potential that predicts exactly 43"/century of precession. No free parameters are adjusted.
5
Constitutive closure: Specify the Sun's mass, Mercury's orbital elements, and the assumption of a vacuum, spherically symmetric exterior. The Schwarzschild metric closes the system.
6
Structural principle: Einstein's field equations are the unique second-order equations for the metric that are divergence-free and reduce to Newtonian gravity in the weak-field limit. The Bianchi identities, ∇μGμν = 0, are not empirical laws. They are structural constraints on admissible geometry. They guarantee that the field equations automatically conserve energy-momentum.
7
Unifying framework: The same geometric structure explains perihelion precession, gravitational lensing, gravitational redshift, frame-dragging, and gravitational wave emission. What began as an orbital irregularity terminates in differential geometry.
The Critical Juncture

When does explanation actually begin?

Not at regularity. Not at fundamental structure alone. Explanation begins when abstract relations are tied to a specified domain via constitutive relations. A fundamental field equation says: if the world has this structure, quantities must satisfy this relation. But it does not say what the material is, how it responds, which regime we are in, or what parameters apply.

Structure + Material Specification + Boundary Conditions = Explanation
Fundamental equations tell you what is possible. Constitutive laws tell you what is actual. Boundary conditions tell you what happens here.

The explanatory work happens when we close the system. An operational law within a specified regime, supplied with equations of state and initial conditions, yields a determinate prediction and thus a concrete explanation. The structural framework above it explains why entire families of such laws share the form they do.

Analogy

The ladder in law

A similar stratification appears in jurisprudence. A particular dispute is observed; it is classified under procedural rules; patterns of adjudication stabilise into doctrine. Civil and criminal codes organise practice. Overriding objectives articulate higher principles guiding interpretation. Statute law, codified and periodically revised, sits above them. Yet uncodified, judge-made law continues to structure application, while constitutional principles constrain what legislation itself may validly prescribe.

In both science and law, explanation deepens as one ascends: from case to rule, from rule to structured code, from code to principle, from principle to the framework that makes the system coherent.

The Structural Moral

Climbing the ladder is not merely adding complexity. It is increasing structural generality. Each step increases abstraction and reduces immediacy, but increases explanatory scope.

What appears initially as a local adjustment becomes intelligible only when embedded in a wider architecture whose internal constraints determine what is even possible.

The Deep Tension

Induction builds the ladder. Deduction descends it.

At every transition between rungs, the Induction Principle is at work. From percept to fact: private perception becomes public report. From fact to regularity: repeated instances justify generalisation. From regularity to law: the pattern is expressed analytically. From law to framework: the law is embedded in a deeper architecture.

But in a Block universe, where all of spacetime exists tenselessly, the explanatory order runs the other way. Structure entails law. Law entails phenomenon. The direction of explanation is deductive downward, even though the direction of discovery is inductive upward.

?
The tension at the heart of the ladder

Induction (climbing up)

Repeated empirical adequacy leads to generalisation, which leads to embedding in deeper structure. This is how we build the ladder. It is iterative, revisable, historically contingent. Each rung is provisional until stabilised by further testing.

Deduction (descending down)

In the finished theory, structure constrains law, law constrains phenomenon. The Bianchi identities guarantee conservation. Gauge symmetry fixes interaction terms. The direction of explanation is top-down, even though the direction of discovery was bottom-up.

Can we legitimately use induction to justify structures that are meant to be timeless constraints? If the block universe is fully deductive, induction seems merely epistemic, a ladder we kick away once we have arrived. But historically, our ascent to structural laws has always been iterative and revisable. Newton's "timeless" law of gravitation turned out to be an approximation. The structural principle that replaced it may itself be provisional.

Open Questions

Rovelli's challenges to the ladder

Carlo Rovelli, among others, identifies three questions of philosophical import in modern fundamental physics that bear directly on the stability of the upper rungs:

1. Do we need to take the observer into account to describe nature?

2. Is physics better formulated in terms of a distinctive reality or what we observe?

3. Does it make sense to think that physical laws themselves might evolve?

The first question challenges the neutrality of rung 1. If the observer is constitutive of what is observed, as relational interpretations of quantum mechanics suggest, then the ladder does not begin with a view from nowhere. The percept is already shaped by the act of measurement.

The second question targets the divide between rungs 4 and 6. Should our laws describe an observer-independent reality (realism), or should they be understood as devices for predicting the outcomes of experiments (instrumentalism)? The metric tensor gμν is extraordinarily useful. Whether it exists is a separate question.

The third question is the most destabilising. If laws evolve, then the distinction between operational law and structural principle is itself historically contingent. What we took to be rung 6 or 7 might be provisional rung 4 or 5. The ladder would not terminate; it would fold back on itself, with today's deepest framework becoming tomorrow's effective description.

!
If laws evolve: consequences for the ladder

What seems stable

Structural principles, symmetries, conservation laws, the Bianchi identities, appear more stable than operational laws. They constrain rather than describe. Their mathematical necessity gives them an air of permanence.

What might be provisional

But even structural principles might be emergent rather than fundamental. In some approaches to quantum gravity, spacetime itself is emergent. If the arena of physics is not given but derived, then even rung 7 is not the top. Operational laws may evolve. Structural principles appear more stable. But even they might be replaced.
