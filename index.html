The rope-ladder of explanation

where description turns into explanation, and then meta-interpretation

Think of inquiry as a rope ladder disappearing into mist. The theorist climbs by grasping successively more general rungs. The “Supreme Court of Appeal” does not supply a final top rung. It decides whether the last rung will hold.

Each ascent is two-way. Induction ties new rungs above from repeated success below. Deduction then climbs back down, deriving what should be seen and measured.

The crucial hinge is not “law” in the abstract. Explanation starts when a quantitative relation constrains outcomes within a domain description → explanation, and becomes determinate only when constitutive relations and conditions close the system. Meta-interpretation begins when you explain why whole families of laws share form explanation → meta.

# Rung Triad verb (expanded) What it contributes
1 Signal / encounter Notice | register | detect Raw appearance. A “something happened” event.
2 Public description Name | classify | record Shared report. The phenomenon is stabilised as an object/event.
3 Regularity Summarise | generalise | correlate Pattern: “when A, typically B.” Still description at scale.
4 Operational law Explain | model | predict description → explanation Quantitative constraint among measured variables within a defined regime.
5 Constitutive closure Specify | close | calibrate Makes laws usable: equation of state, response law, plus boundary/initial conditions. Converts “formal” into “determinate”.
6 Structural constraint Constrain | derive | legitimise Why admissible laws must have certain forms (symmetry, invariance, conservation).
7 Unifying framework Interpret | unify | re-express explanation → meta Meta-level coherence. Many domain-laws become one architecture in different regimes.

Minimal rule: Explanation is not “a fundamental equation” alone. It is operational constraint plus constitutive closure, then tested by the court of measurement. Meta begins when you explain why the constraints come in families.
